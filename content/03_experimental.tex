First, the sample stage is placed at the middle point of a 3D Helmholtz coil setup consisting of pairs in $x$, $y$, and $z$ directions.
Due to the three coils mounted in the setup, a transport in $x$ and $y$ directions is possible and, therefore, all coil components will be introduced.
However, throughout this work, transport was performed only along $x$, for which reason the scheme in Figure \ref{fig:exp_setup} a) is simplified down to two Helmholtz pairs in $x$ and $z$ direction, and, consequently, the pulsed fields depicted in b) are for those two coils. %magnetic field directions?

The coil pairs are individually driven by a power supply and addressed by a LabVIEW program via a digital input/output device from National Instruments, coded by Huhnstock \cite{HuhnstockThesis}.
Trapizoidal fields depicted in Figure \ref{fig:exp_setup} b) are created with a linear rise time $\Delta t_\mathrm{r}$, a plateau time $\Delta t_\mathrm{p}$, and a linear drop time $\Delta t_\mathrm{d}$ \cite{HuhnstockThesis}.
Because of the high magnetic field alteration rate of $V_\mathrm{H}= \SI{3.2e6}{\A\per(\m\s)}$, the rise and drop time intervals of the trapezoidal magnetic field pulses are short compared to the plateau time \cite{Holzinger2015a}; in the rise and drop times the particle might move a maximum distance of \SI{20}{\nano\m} with \SI{5}{\micro\m} stripe widths, therefore, the transport will neglect rise and fall times and will be referring to the plateau times only \cite{Holzinger2015a}.
As shown in Figure \ref{fig:exp_setup} b), the transport scheme uses periodic alternations of $H_\mathrm{x\,max}$ and $H_\mathrm{z\,max}$ fields, where $H_\mathrm{x}$ and $H_\mathrm{z}$ are phase shifted by $\nicefrac{\pi}{2}$ or $\nicefrac{T}{4}$ \cite{Holzinger2015a}.
This was also realized by the LabVIEW program.

The coil setup is then placed under a Zeiss Axiotop reflective microscope, capable of bright-field and dark-field modes.
This is important as the samples are opaque and a traditional transmissive microscope won't work.
The microscope was fitted with a $20\,\times$ and a $50\,\times$ objective depending on the experiment conducted. 
In detail, these are the Zeiss EC Epiplan $20\,\times$ with a numerical aperture $\mathrm{NA} = 0.4$, and the Zeiss EC Epiplan-Neofluar $50\,\times$ with a numerical aperture $\mathrm{NA} = 0.8$, both suited for dark-field microscopy.

Finally, to record the experiments, the high-speed camera EoSense 2.0 MCX12-CM from Microtron was mounted on top. 
This monochromatic camera with a $\SI{19.2}{\milli\m} \times \SI{10.8}{\milli\m}$ sensor hosting $\SI{10}{\micro \m} \times \SI{10}{\micro \m}$ large pixles, is capable of recording \SI{2247}{frames \per\s} at a maximum resolution of $1920\times 1080$ pixel.
With a structure of known dimensions, a conversion factor from pixel to \si{\micro\m} was calculated to be \SI{0.5}{\micro \m \per pixel} and \SI{0.2}{\micro \m \per pixel} for the $20\,\times$ and $50\,\times$ objectives, respectively.
Data acquisition is done via the frame grabber FireBird CoaXPress from Active Silicon installed in the acquisition PC, connecting the camera with four micro-BNC cables. 
Note that the maximum acquisition frame rate of the frame grabber at hand is limited to \SI{1000}{frames \per\s}.


As shown in Figure \ref{fig:exp_setup} a), the transport substrate, or sample, is illuminated from the top.
It consists of the magnetic layer stack presented in Figure \ref{fig:exp_setup} c), which is $\SI{1}{\centi\m}^2$ or $\SI{1.5}{\centi\m}^2$, with an additional frame-like cutout of Parafilm that is placed on top of the spacer layer to contain the particle suspension. %#TODO abbildung 
Finally, a cover slip is placed on top, sealing the liquid inside what is then called a microfluidic chamber, where the chamber sidewalls are determined by the \SI{120}{\micro\m} thick Parafilm. 
This is crucial for two main reasons: 
First, the minute volume of liquid in the \SIrange{10}{20}{\micro\liter} range won't evaporate as quickly, allowing for experiments to take place for periods of time in the one to two hour regime. 
Second, due to the hydrophobic nature of the spacer, the liquid will immediately form a droplet that acts like a lens, warping the microscopic image. 
With the cover slip arrangement, the liquid is pressed flat. 
The aforementioned spacer can be sputtered Si after the fabrication of the transport substrate, or it can be a polymer that is spin-coated, producing suitable thicknesses that depend on the spin coating parameters.

\section{Transport substrate preparation}
To create the transport substrates, a \SIrange{15}{15}{\milli\m} naturally oxidized Si substrate is cleaned with isopropanol and dried in a stream of \ch{N2} before placing it in an \ac{RF} sputtering machine. 
There, Ar plasma is ignited while a magnetic field is applied.
...
Films of x,y,z nm are sputtered onto the Si substrate creating the magnetic layer stack.
This happened in the presense of a magnetic field of x mT, giving the initial direction of the magnetic moments of the ferromagnet.

The substrate is then placed in a small furnace that heats the sample above the Neel temperature and while keeping below the curie temperature, freely rotating the AF.
As there is a magnetic field in the direction of the magneic moment of the FM, the topmost layer of atoms of the AF allign to the direction of the FM, creating a pinning called exchange coupling.

The substrate is then covered with a lithographic mask which, in this case, masekd 5µm thin stripes.
After developing  in xy, the substrate is placed in a homemade He Ion blaster. 
This introduces both energy and defect in the unmasked portion of the substrate, allowing the magnetic moments to rotate.
While this is happening, a magnetic field opposing the magentic moment of the substrate then turns the now freed magnetic moments. 
After He irradiation, the magentic moments are now pinned by the Af in the oppoisng directoin, creating the stripe domain pattern we know and love. 
\subsection{Thin film sputter deposition}
To achieve high-quality thin films with monolayer accuracy, sputter deposition is most commonly used\cite{Seshan2012}.    
Sputtering, in a nutshell, is the process of accelerating ionized atoms, often \ac{Ar}, into a surface of desired material referred to as the target, which can be metals or insulators, with high enough kinetic energy to physically remove some target atoms and condense them onto a substrate.
\ch{Ar} is usually used as it is cost-effective, chemically inert, and its atomic mass is in the same order as desired metals that are used in thin film applications\cite{Seshan2012}.  
Since the bombardment of ions physically removes target atoms, this technique is classified as a \ac{PVD} technique\cite{Seshan2012}. 
However, compared to evaporation, sputter deposition produces a high-energy flux with high surface mobility, allowing atoms to condense into smooth, dense, conformal, and continuous films more easily than evaporated films\cite{Seshan2012}.
When non-inert gases, such as oxygen or nitrogen, are used, the process is then called reactive sputtering, which is typically used to deposit oxides or nitrides\cite{Seshan2012}.

Linked to the apparatus used, three main sputtering mechanisms are available: \ac{DC} diode sputtering, \ac{RF} sputtering, and magnetron sputtering.
They all share the same core principles, and the aufbau is similar, which can be seen in Figure x. 
For \ac{DC} diode sputtering, two plates working as anode and cathode connected to a power supply are placed in a vacuum.
The anode is composed of the substrate to be coated, and the cathode is the material to be sputtered\cite{Frey2015}.
\ac{Ar}, as the typical sputtering gas, is introduced in the pressure range of \SI{e-3}{mbar}, and a voltage across the plates is applied\cite{Seshan2012}.  
Above a certain breakdown voltage, a plasma is ignited, creating free electrons and accelerating \ch{Ar+} towards the negative cathode in the electric field.
Since the electrons are much lighter than the ions, they experience a much higher velocity, which results in a thin sheath layer next to the cathode, an area of electron depletion causing most of the potential drop between anode and cathode\cite{Seshan2012}. 
There, the acceleration of the ions is greatest, right befor they impact into the target\cite{Paetzold2004}. 
Secondary electrons are created upon impact, which are accelerated through the sheath layer towards the anode, ionizing more \ac{Ar} in the path, which sustains the plasma.   
Electrons that do not collide with \ch{Ar} are absorbed by the anode and the apparatus walls, not contributing to ionization\cite{Seshan2012}.
Generally, the sputter yield is proportional to the power supplied to create the plasma.
Ionization cross section peaks for electron energies of about \SI{100}{eV} and then drops, meaning that the process cannot be scaled by simply applying more power\cite{Seshan2012}. 
Additionally, in \ac{DC} diode sputtering, only electrically conductive materials can be used as target materials, as the buildup of positive charge of the \ch{Ar+}-ions could not be dissipated, which would lead to the gradual loss of the accelerating potential, terminating the sputtering altogether\cite{Paetzold2004}. 
For this reason, \ac{RF} sputtering was utilized in this work.

In \ac{RF} sputtering, the \ac{DC} power supply is replaced by an \ac{AC} \ac{RF} source\cite{Seshan2012}.
The supplied \ac{RF} is typically \SI{13.56}{MHz} or some multiple\cite{Seshan2012}.
This \ac{RF} power couples to the electron motion in the plasma, resulting in longer residence times in the plasma, higher collisional ionization, and higher plasma densities\cite{Seshan2012}.
The accelerating potential buildup has a different mechanism than the \ac{DC} diode setup and heavily depends on the different masses of electrons and \ch{Ar+}-ions.
During a given positive half-period of the alternating voltage, more electrons are able to reach the target than ions during a negative half-period\cite{Paetzold2004}.
Since the target is coupled to a capacitor plate, the electrons cannot dissipate\cite{Paetzold2004}.
On the other side, during a negative half-period, electrons that reach the substrate can dissipate as the substrate is connected to ground\cite{Paetzold2004}. 
This means that the target builds up a negative charge over time\cite{Paetzold2004}. 
The result is a so-called self-bias \ac{DC} voltage that ensures the quasi-neutrality of the plasma, where electrons are retarded while ions are accelerated\cite {Paetzold2004}.
To maximize the energy transfer, impedance-matching hardware is installed to tune the output impedance of the power supply to impedance-match the plasma impedance\cite{Seshan2012}.
Additionally, the \ac{RF} sputtering allows for sputtering insulators such as silicon dioxide.
This is due to the \ac{AC} applied to the diodes, preventing charge buildup\cite{Seshan2012}.

\subsection{Dynamic light scattering: Zeta potential}
Zeta potential measurements were carried out using a Zetasizer Nano ZS90 from Malvern Instruments Ltd, which uses an electric field to accelerate charged particles, while a laser of known frequency shines onto the suspension.
\SI{17}{\degree} angled to the normal of the laser beam is a detector that measures the dynamically scattered light, i.e., during the acceleration of the particles towards one of the two poles.
The apparatus continuously measures up to 100 times, until the obtained value converges and is therefore accurate enough. 
Since the temperature changes the viscosity of the medium, the cuvette is kept at \SI{25}{\celsius} during the measurement. 
Measurement is then conducted three times, which leads to an overall accurate value. 

Zeta potentials were measured for a variety of particles and solution combinations.

\section{BSA protocol}
\acp{MP} that are designed to bind to proteins such as \ac{BSA} are, for example, Thermo Fisher Dynabeads™ M-270 Carboxylic Acid.
Carboxylic acid, as the particle's functional surface group, can form an amide bond with a primary amino group of the protein.
The concentration of the stock suspension of the M-270 Carboxylic Acid beads is $c=\SI{2e9}{beads\per(\milli\L))}$.

For binding \ac{BSA} to the M-270 Carboxylic Acid particles, the coupling protocol of the manufacturer was followed and slightly tweaked.
It consists of a one-step coating procedure with several washing steps and is mediated by carbodiimide activation.

\SI{40}{\micro\L} \ac{HEPES} was added again to repeat the washing three times.
4. The third time supernatant is removed, the \acp{MP} are resuspended in \SI{50}{\micro\L} BSA solution of \SI{0.1}{\m\g\per(\m\L)} concentration with \SI{20}{\micro\L} \ac{HEPES}.

\section{DLVO-Forces}\label{sec:theory_dlvo}
\acp{MP} for the use in a \ac{LOC} system are suspended in a liquid, usually a buffer or distilled water. 
Therefore, \ac{DLVO} forces are of utter importance\cite{Shubbak2026}.
These interactions are named after their discoverer Derjaguin, Landau, Verwey, and Overbeck \cite{Derjaguin1941, Verwey1947, WirixSpeetjens2005}. 
For the system and hand, two main liquid-mediated interactions are of importance: the electrostatic and Van der Waals interactions, which will be briefly introduced in the following sections. 


\subsection{Electrostatic intercations}
The origin of the electrostatic interaction of colloidal systems such as \acp{MP} in an aqueous solution is the surface charge that builds up at the surface \cite{Park2011}.
This is illustrated in Figure \ref{fig:double_layer}, where an \ac{MP} is suspended in a liquid above a polymer surface as the spacing layer of the transport substrate.
\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=.9\linewidth]{Abbildungen/Theory/ElectricalDoubleLayer.png}
    \caption{
            Diagram of an electric double layer of an \ac{MP}.
            The \ac{MP} is suspended in a liquid on top of the substrate.
            Positive surface charges are compensated by anions that then make up the Stern layer that is firmly adhering to the surface.
            This layer is further compensated by cations that loosely surround the first layer, which then make up the slipping plane.
            $\zeta$-potential is measured at the slipping plane.
            Ions adhered to the \ac{PMMA} surface are drawn faintly as to guide the reader's eye to the \ac{MP}.
            Inspired by \cite{Park2011}.%page 47
            }
    \label{fig:double_layer}
\end{figure}
The electrical neutral plane reaching away from the surface after all charges have been compensated is called the electrical double layer and includes the Stern layer and the diffuse layer, where the former is tightly bound ions with opposing charge to the surface charge they are adsorbed to, and the latter is more loosely surrounding the first layer \cite{Park2011}.  
The electrical double layer is depicted in Figure \ref{fig:double_layer} with a purple arrow.
The blue arrow shows the diffuse layer, which incorporates the loosely bound ions, counteracting the Stern layer.  
Note that the \ac{PMMA} surface also has its electrical double layer; ions constituting the Stern layer and slipping plane are depicted faintly in Figure \ref{fig:double_layer} so as not to overwhelm the scheme.  
When both systems, that is, two particles or the particle and the substrate's surface, are in such proximity that their individual electrical double layers overlap, an interaction occurs.
Whether this interaction is attractive or repulsive depends on the sign of the charge.
This interaction can be described by
\begin{equation}\label{eq:F_electrostatic}
    \mathbf{F}_\mathrm{el}(z,t)= \frac{2\pi \epsilon_\mathrm{0} \epsilon_\mathrm{r} r \kappa}{1 - \exp{(-2\kappa z)}} \left[ 2 \zeta_\mathrm{MP} \zeta_\mathrm{s}  \exp{(-\kappa z)} \pm (\zeta_\mathrm{MP}^2 + \zeta_\mathrm{s}^2) \exp{(-2 \kappa z)} \right]\mathbf{e}_\mathrm{z},
\end{equation}
where $\epsilon_\mathrm{0} \epsilon_\mathrm{r}$ is the permittivity of the liquid medium, $r$ is the particles radius, and $\kappa$ is the  Debye length
\begin{equation}
    \kappa = \sqrt{\frac{2000 N_{\mathrm{A}} e^2 I}{\epsilon_{\mathrm{0}} \epsilon_{\mathrm{r}} k_{\mathrm{B}}T}},
\end{equation}
with the Avogadro constant $N_{\mathrm{A}}$, the elementary charge $e$, the ionic strength of the electrolyte $I$, the Boltzmann constant $k_{\mathrm{B}}$ and temperature $T$ \cite{WirixSpeetjens2005, Reginka2021}.
Since the surface charge is experimentally hard to determine, the zeta potential $\zeta_{\mathrm{MP}}$ and $\zeta_{\mathrm{s}}$ of \ac{MP} and substrate surfaces are commonly used as an approximation instead \cite{WirixSpeetjens2005, Ehresmann2015, Reginka2021} and is measurerd at the slipping plane, see Figure \ref{fig:double_layer}.

\subsection{Close to surface transport}
Using relatively weak magnetic fields in the range of \SIrange{1}{4}{\kilo\A\per\m}, the superparamagnetic bead's magentic moment $\mathbf{m_\mathrm{MP}}$ depends on the external magnetic field linearly as 
\begin{equation}\label{eq:m_MP}
    \mathbf{m_\mathrm{MP}} = \bar{\chi}_\mathrm{MP} V \mathbf{H}, 
\end{equation}
where $V$ is the bead's volume, and 
\begin{equation}\label{eq:effective_suceptibility_MP}
    \bar{\chi}=\frac{3\chi_\mathrm{MP}}{\chi_\mathrm{MP} + 3} 
\end{equation}
is the shape corrected bead susceptibility\cite{Yellen2007}.
Here, the \ac{MP} is treated as a point dipole, which is a reasonable approximation when the magnetic field does not vary drastically across the \ac{MP}'s volume, but will be discussed later \cite{Yellen2007}.


When the \ac{MP} sees the magnetic field emerging from the \ac{MFL}, the potential energy $U_\mathrm{P}$ of the \ac{MP} at lateral position $x$ and above the substrate $z$ is given by\cite{Holzinger2015a} 
\begin{equation}\label{eq:U_P_MP}
    U_\mathrm{P}(x,z) = -\mu_0 \mathbf{m_\mathrm{MP}}(x,z) \mathbf{H}(x,z), 
\end{equation}
where $\mu_0$ is the vacuum permeability.
It becomes apparent that $U_\mathrm{P}(x,z)$ is smallest where $\mathbf{H}(x,z)$ is largest, which is where the magnetic stray fields emerge from the substrate, that is, at the \acp{DW}.

The magnetic force exerted on the superparamagnetic bead in the direction of motion $x$ is given by 
\begin{equation}
    F_\mathrm{m,x}(x,z,t)=V(\chi_\mathrm{MP}-\chi_\mathrm{fl}) \mu_0  ((\mathbf{H}_\mathrm{eff} \mathbf{\nabla}) \mathbf{H}_\mathrm{eff})_\mathrm{x},
\end{equation}
where $\chi_\mathrm{fl}$ is the susceptibility of the fluid, and $\mathbf{H}_\mathrm{eff}$ is the effective field, which is the sum of the static stray field emerging from the \ac{hh}-\ac{tt}-\ac{DW} that are responsible for the magnetic gradient, and superposed external fields created by the Helmholtz coils, at the position of the particle\cite{Shubbak2026}.

\paragraph{Rotating Field Traveling Wave Magnetophoresis} 

Magnetic fields produced by Helmholtz coils in this work are of a pulsed nature with a trapezoidal shape (Figure \ref{fig:exp_setup} (b)); however, treating the fields as rotating fields will be the starting point of the derivation before introducing the consequences of deviating to pulses.
The static $H_\mathrm{MFL}$ produced by the substrate and the time-dependent homogeneous rotating external magnetic fields $H_\mathrm{ext}$ produced by the Helmholz coils result in effective fields at position $x,y,z_0$ and time $t$ and are given by \cite{Ehresmann2026}
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{H}_\mathrm{eff}(x,y,z_0,t) = \mathbf{H}_\mathrm{MFL}(x,y,z_0) + \mathbf{H}_\mathrm{ext}(t).
\end{equation}
For the present system, which is depicted in Figure \ref{fig:exp_setup}, the \ac{MFL} is periodic along the $x$-direction and the external fields rotate in the $xz$-plane, giving an effective field that can be modeled using trigonometric functions \cite{Yellen2007, Ehresmann2026} 
\begin{equation}\label{eq:H_eff_approximation_yellen}
    \mathbf{H}_\mathrm{eff}(x,y,z_0,t) = \begin{bmatrix} 
                        H_\mathrm{eff,x}  \\ 
                        H_\mathrm{eff,y} \\
                        H_\mathrm{eff,z}
                    \end{bmatrix} = 
                    \begin{bmatrix} 
                        A(z_0)\sin(kx) +H_\mathrm{ext,0} \sin(\omega t) \\ 
                        0 \\
                        A(z_0)\cos(kx) +H_\mathrm{ext,0} \cos(\omega t)  
                    \end{bmatrix},
\end{equation}
where $k = \nicefrac{2\pi}{d}$ is the reciprocal lattice number of the periodic \ac{MFL}, periodic in one dimension with period length $d$, $\omega=2\pi\nu$ the angular freqnecy of the external rotating field, 
\begin{equation}\label{Eq:heuristic_A}    
A=\nicefrac{\sigma_0}{2}\exp(-2\pi \beta)
\end{equation}
is a heuristic expression containing $\beta = \nicefrac{r}{d}$ the ratio between the underlying period $d$ and the radius of the \ac{MP} $r$, and $\nicefrac{\sigma_0}{2}$, which describes the stray field amplitude $H_\mathrm{MFL}(0,z)=0$ at the surface of the \ac{FM} layer (Figure \ref{fig:exp_setup}), with the exponential decrease of the fields with height $z$ \cite{Yellen2007, Ehresmann2026}.  
In other terms, $A(0,0,z_0)$ denotes the amplitude of the periodic \ac{MFL} at a distance $z_0$ of the \ac{MP} center to the surface of the \ac{FM} layer, when \ac{MP} radii $r\gg z_0$, which is true when the spacer layer thickness $t_\mathrm{spacer}< \SI{1}{\micro\m}$ (see Figure \ref{fig:exp_setup}).
With that, the height dependence of the parallel-stripe domains can be evaluated to replace the heuristic expression in Equation \ref{Eq:heuristic_A}.
As given by Equation \ref{eq:U_P_MP}, the \ac{MP} resides above a \ac{hh} or \ac{tt} \ac{DW}; for the sake of this derivation, it shall be above a \ac{hh} \ac{DW}.
The $x$-components of the \ac{MFL} across the \ac{MP} average out\cite{Ehresmann2026}.
As known from literature \cite{Zingsem2017}, the absolute magnitude of the magnetic field of an infinitely thin \ac{hh} \ac{DW} stretching infinitely in $y$-direction as a function of height $z$ directly above a \ac{DW} can be approximated by
\begin{equation}
    \lvert \mathbf{H}_\mathrm{MFL}(0,0,z_0) \rvert = \lvert \mathbf{H}_\mathrm{MFL}^\mathrm{line}(0,0,z_0) \rvert = H_\mathrm{MFL, z}^\mathrm{line} = \frac{\eta_0}{2\pi z_0},
\end{equation}
where $\eta_0 = 2M_\mathrm{s}t_\mathrm{FM}$ describes the magnetic charge density of the \ac{DW} with the saturation magnetisation $M_\mathrm{S}$ and the thickness $t_\mathrm{FM}$ of the \ac{FM}. 
The superposition of adjacent \acp{DW} reduces the stray field over a \ac{DW}, which is modeled by\cite{Ehresmann2026}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fields_of_adjecent_DW}
    \lvert \mathbf{H}_\mathrm{MFL, z}^\mathrm{line}(0,0,z_0) \rvert = \frac{2M_\mathrm{s}t_\mathrm{FM}}{2\pi}\left(\frac{1}{z_0}-\frac{2}{\sqrt{z^2_0 +\left( \frac{d}{2}\right)^2}}+\frac{2}{{\sqrt{z^2_0 +4\left( \frac{d}{2}\right)^2}}} - \frac{4}{d} (\ln(2) - 0.5)\right).
\end{equation}

As briefly mentioned before, the \ac{MP} will be approximated as a point-dipole.
To describe the magnetic moment of the \ac{MP} is, however, not trivial.
The particle consists of nanoscopic maghemite or magnetite or a mixture of both that is either dispersed evenly within a polymer matrix of the sphere, or the grains are surrounding a polymer core, in which case the particle is referred to as a core-shell particle. 
The particles investigated in this work are Dynabeads from Thermo Fisher, which are, as specified by the manufacturer, non-porous, highly cross-linked polystyrene microspheres consisting of an even dispersion of magnetic material throughout the bead, and micromer-M from micromod, which are microspheres consisting of magnetite around an organic matrix of a styrene-maleic acid-copolymer further coated with an acrylic acid derivative polymer\cite{Shubbak2026}, making the latter a core-shell particle (depicted in Figure \ref{fig:exp_setup} c)).
In both cases, the individual grains are only talking to each other if they are close enough, that is, in the order of xy for exchange interaction or in the order of yz for dipolar interaction. 
This interaction, or the lack thereof, is what makes the particles superparamagnetic. 
For a complete description of the magnetic moment of an \ac{MP}, an integration of individual magnetic field components of the dispersed grains over the volume with respect to the decaying magnetic stray field emerging from the \ac{MFL} must be considered.  
Complicated as this already is, the movement of the \acp{MP} in the transport concept is not constant along $z$ \cite{Huhnstock2022} and therefore, the effective magnetic moment of each \ac{MP} is also time dependent.

As this is outside the scope of this work, a point-dipole will be assumed for both particle types, i.e., matrix-dispersed and core-shell particles.
Nevertheless, the field component varying along $z$ at location $x = 0$ of the \ac{MP} will be included to better approximate the current system by using a dimensionless weight factor $w(rk)$ which depends on the ratio $\beta$\cite{Ehresmann2026}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:weight_factor_dipole_approx}
    w(rk) = w\left(\frac{2\pi r}{d}\right) = \frac{2 \int_0^r \frac{r^2-x^2}{r^2}\cos(kx)dx}{2 \int_0^r \frac{r^2-x^2}{r^2}dx} = \frac{\frac{2(\sin(rk)-rk\cos(rk))}{r^2k^3}}{\frac{2r}{3}}=\frac{3(\sin(rk)-rk\cos(rk))}{r^3k^3}, 
\end{equation}
where $\frac{r^2-x^2}{r^2}$ represents the area of circular slices of a sphere varying with $x$.  
%include figure for this funciton
From Equation \ref{eq:weight_factor_dipole_approx} follows, that the smaller $\beta$, the larger the average fields across the volume.
This is depicted in Figure x for $d = 5,10$ and $\SI{20}{\micro\meter}$.
Including Equation \ref{eq:weight_factor_dipole_approx} into Equation \ref{eq:fields_of_adjecent_DW} gives the volume-averaged field acting on the center of the \ac{MP} to induce its magentic moment by\cite{Ehresmann2026}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:H_eff_Arno}
    \mathbf{H}_\mathrm{MFL}(x,y,z_0) = \begin{bmatrix}
                                        H_\mathrm{MFL, x} \\
                                        0 \\
                                        H_\mathrm{MFL, z}
                                    \end{bmatrix} 
                            =
                                    \begin{bmatrix}
                                        \rvert H_\mathrm{MFL,z}^\mathrm{line}(0,0,z_0)\lvert\cdot w(rk)\sin(kx)\\
                                        0 \\
                                        \rvert H_\mathrm{MFL,z}^\mathrm{line}(0,0,z_0)\lvert\cdot w(rk)\cos(kx)
                                    \end{bmatrix}
                                    :=
                                    \begin{bmatrix}
                                        B\sin(kx) \\
                                        0 \\
                                        B\cos(kx)
                                    \end{bmatrix}.
\end{equation}
Equation \ref{eq:H_eff_approximation_yellen} is therefore replaced by Equation \ref{eq:H_eff_Arno} as Equation \ref{eq:H_eff_Arno} more adequately describes the effective rotating fields acting on the \ac{MP} in the current geometry.   
With the dipole approximation for an \ac{MP}, inserting Equations \ref{eq:m_MP} and \ref{eq:effective_suceptibility_MP} into Equation \ref{eq:U_P_MP} gives \cite{Ehresmann2026}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:potential_energy_with_H}
    U_\mathrm{P}(x,z,t) = -\bar{\chi}V\mu_0\left( H^2_\mathrm{eff, x} + H^2_\mathrm{eff, z} \right).
\end{equation}
Inserting Equation \ref{eq:H_eff_Arno} gives\cite{Ehresmann2026}
\begin{equation}
            U_\mathrm{P}(x,z,t) = -\bar{\chi}V\mu_0\left( \left( B \sin(kx)+H_\mathrm{ext,0} \sin(\omega t)\right)^2 + \left(B \sin(kx)+H_\mathrm{ext,0} \cos(\omega t)\right)^2\right).
\end{equation}
After resolving the brackets, using the Pythagorean identity of $\sin^2+\cos^2 = 1$ and applying the addition theorem on the product terms of the trigonometric functions, which can all be read in detail elsewhere \cite{Ehresmann2026}, finally gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:dPEL_Rotating}
     U_\mathrm{P}(x,z,t) = -\bar{\chi}V\mu_0 \left(B^2 + H^2_\mathrm{ext,0}+BH_\mathrm{ext,0}\cos(kx-\omega t)\right).
\end{equation}
This describes a dynamic potential energy landscape with minima and maxima traveling in the $\pm x$-direction.
Since the potential energy of the \ac{MP} $U_\mathrm{P}$ depends on $x$, and the magnetic field changes with $x$, a spatial change in energy means the \ac{MP} experiences a force.
Using Equation \ref{eq:potential_energy_with_H}, therefore, gives 
\begin{equation}
    F_\mathrm{x}(x,z,t) = -\frac{\partial U(x,z,t)}{\partial x} = +\bar{\chi}V\mu_0 BkH_\mathrm{ext,0}\sin(kx-\omega t). 
\end{equation}
The in-synergy traveling potential energy minima and maxima are responsible for the potential energy well-depth and magnetic gradient force on the \ac{MP} in Equation \ref{eq:dPEL_Rotating} do not show a time variation.
The potential energy well-depths are proportional to the amplitude of the external field and the stray field averaged over the particle volume corrected by the weight factor \ref{eq:weight_factor_dipole_approx}. 
Since the \ac{MP} is residing in the potential minimum in accordance with \ref{eq:potential_energy_with_H}, the acting force along $x$ moves the \ac{MP} accordingly. 
Fittingly, the transport concept is coined Traveling Wave Magnetophoresis by Yellen and Lee in 2007 \cite{Yellen2007}, although no wave is involved at all.

\paragraph{Pulsed Field Traveling Wave Magnetophoresis} 

In this work, the parallel-stripe domain pattern extends in $y$ direction while alternating in $x$ with the half-period $d$, meaning no gradient is created in $y$, which ensures that the force only acts along the $x$ direction\cite{Holzinger2015a}. 
Furthermore, the Ansatz for rotating fields is used as a starting point for the derivation of the dynamically shifting potential energy landscape, although pulsed fields are employed in the experimental scheme \cite{Holzinger2015a, Shubbak2026}, which is depicted in Figure \ref{fig:exp_setup} (b).
Rewriting Equation \ref{eq:H_eff_Arno} in a more general form to include variations in pulsed fields\cite{Ehresmann2026} and replacing the angular frequency by a frequency $\nu = \nicefrac{\omega}{2\pi}$\cite{Shubbak2026} yields
\begin{equation}\label{eq:pulsed_dTWM}
    \mathbf{H}_\mathrm{eff}(x,y,z_0,t) = \begin{bmatrix}
        B \sin(kx) + H_\mathrm{ext,x,0}\sin(2\pi\nu_\mathrm{x} t) \\
        0 \\
        B \cos(kx) + H_\mathrm{ext,x,0}\sin(2\pi\nu_\mathrm{z} t + \delta_\mathrm{zx})
    \end{bmatrix}.
\end{equation}
As Equation \ref{eq:pulsed_dTWM} implies and shown in Figure \ref{fig:exp_setup} (b), different pulse amplitudes in $H_\mathrm{ext,x,0}$ and $H_\mathrm{ext,z,0}$ are possible, as well as differeing the frequencies $\nu_\mathrm{x}$ and $\nu_\mathrm{z}$ and the phase relation $\delta_\mathrm{zx}$ between them. 
For this work, $\delta_\mathrm{zx}$ was kept at a constant $\SI{90}{\degree}$ shift, simplefieing Equation \ref{eq:pulsed_dTWM} to 
\begin{equation}\label{eq:pulsed_dTWM_no_phase_variation}
    \mathbf{H}_\mathrm{eff}(x,y,z_0,t) = \begin{bmatrix}
        B \sin(kx) + H_\mathrm{ext,x,0}\sin(2\pi\nu_\mathrm{x} t) \\
        0 \\
        B \cos(kx) + H_\mathrm{ext,x,0}\cos(2\pi\nu_\mathrm{z} t )
    \end{bmatrix}.
\end{equation}
With Equation \ref{eq:pulsed_dTWM_no_phase_variation}, the dynamic potential energy landscape for pulsed fields can be written as\cite{Ehresmann2026}
\begin{equation}
    U_\mathrm{P}(x,z,t) = -\bar{\chi}V\mu_0 (B\sin(kx) + H_\mathrm{ext,x,0}\sin(2\pi\nu t))^2. 
\end{equation}


Using an equation of motion, the transport from one \ac{DW} to the adjacent by periodically transforming $U_\mathrm{P}(x,z,t)$ can be described by\cite{Yellen2007, Shubbak2026}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Equation_of_motion}
    M \frac{d^2x}{dt^2}-f_\mathrm{D}\frac{dx}{dt} = F_\mathrm{m,x} \sin(\phi),
\end{equation}
where $M$ is the mass of an \ac{MP}, and the phase $\phi = kx-\omega t$ denotes the relative position of the \ac{MP} with respect to the position of the nearest energy minimum. 
The $z$-height dependent viscous drag coefficient $f_{\mathrm{D}}(z)$ can be written as \cite{WirixSpeetjens2005}:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:f_D}
   f_\mathrm{D}(z)= \left[ 1 - \frac{9}{16} \left( \frac{r}{r+z} \right) + \frac{1}{8}\left( \frac{r}{r+z} \right)^3 - \frac{45}{256}\left( \frac{r}{r+z} \right)^4 - \frac{1}{16} \left( \frac{r}{r+z} \right)^5 \right]^{-1}.
\end{equation}
Here, $z$ is the closest surface-to-surface distance between \ac{MP} and substrate \cite{WirixSpeetjens2005}. %this is during a hopping 100 nm at closest point and 1 µm at furhterst, making the drag time dependant as mp hoppes from DW to DW.
$f_\mathrm{D}(z)$ decays strongly from the substrate's surface into the liquid, which is depicted in Figure \ref{fig:drag_coefficient}, and can reach values between 1, where the \ac{MP} is far away from any wall, and 3, where the \ac{MP} is touching the surface \cite{Holzinger2015a}.

            Drag coefficient as a function of distance $z$ between the substrate surface and the lower surface of the \ac{MP} facing the substrate.
            Different \ac{MP} radii are shown by different colors and lines, where it becomes apparent that the smaller the \ac{MP}, the more sensitive it is to changes in $z$ with respect to the felt drag.
            Figure taken from \cite{Shubbak2026}.
            }
    \label{fig:drag_coefficient}
\end{figure}

Drag force resulting from Stoke's drag of a sphere \cite{Yellen2007} $\mathbf{F}_\mathrm{D}$ is then opposing the magnetic force as \cite{WirixSpeetjens2005}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:drag_force}
    \mathbf{F}_{\mathrm{D}}(z,t) = -6\pi r \eta \frac{d\mathbf{x}}{dt} f_\mathrm{D}(z),  
\end{equation}
where $r$ is the \ac{MP}'s radius, and $\eta$ is the liquid's viscosity which for water at $T=\SI{293.15}{\K}$ is $\SI{1.0093e-3}{\Pa\s}$\cite{Ehresmann2015}).

Since the volumes used in this \ac{LOC} device are in the \si{\micro\L} range, laminar flow occurs, which is described by a low Reynolds number \cite{Shanko2019}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Reynolds_number}
    \mathrm{Re} = \frac{\rho u L}{\mu},
\end{equation}
where at room temperature, $\rho=\SI{1000}{\kg\per\cubic\meter}$ is the density of water, $u$ is the flow speed which, for our system is the velocity of the \acp{MP} and is generously given by \SI{200}{\micro\m\per\s}, $L$ is the characteristic length, which is $<\mathrm{cm}$ range, and $\mu$ is the dynamic viscosity, given at \SI{1e-3}{\Pa}. 
It becomes apparent that at small volumes used, in the present case in the \SI{20}{\micro\L} range, $\mathrm{Re}$ becomes $<1$\cite{Shanko2019}.
This allows for the inertial term of the \ac{MP} to be ignored, and Equation \ref{eq:Equation_of_motion} can be rewritten in a dimensionless form as an overdamped non-linear oscillator\cite{Yellen2007}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:overdamped_oscillator}
    \frac{d\phi}{d\tau}=\sin(\phi)-\frac{\nu}{\nu_\mathrm{c}},
\end{equation}
where $\nu$ is the external pulsed field frequency, $\nu_\mathrm{c} = F_\mathrm{m,x}(x,z,t) k/f_\mathrm{D}(z)$ is the critical frequency for a given \ac{MP} that strongly depends on the material and geometry, and $\tau=\nu_\mathrm{c}t$ is a dimensionless time \cite{Yellen2007}. 
% Equation \ref{eq:overdamped_oscillator} has a stable solution $\frac{d\phi}{d\tau}=0$ only when the driving frequency of the external magnetic field is less than $\omega_\mathrm{c}$ \cite{Yellen2007}.
% Using pulsed fields rather than rotating fields as introduced in Equation \ref{eq:pulsed_dTWM_no_phase_variation}, i.e., replacing $\nu = \nicefrac{\omega}{2\pi}$, and 
With the drag force in Equation \ref{eq:drag_force} as the sole contributor to the damping, $\nu_\mathrm{c}$ can be formulated as:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:critical_frequency}
    \nu_\mathrm{c}(z) = \frac{\bar{\chi} \,\mu_0 \, \sigma_0 \,H_\mathrm{eff}}{36 \pi \,\eta \, f_\mathrm{D}(z)} \left( 2 \pi \beta \right)^2 \exp(-2\pi \beta),
\end{equation}
where $\bar{\chi}$ is the shape-corrected \ac{MP} susceptibility, $\sigma_0$ denotes the effective magnetic pole distribution within the underlying substrate, and $\beta = r/d$ is the ratio between the \ac{MP} radius $r$ and the domain periodicity $d$ \cite{Shubbak2026}.
For \acp{MP} with a radius smaller than the period $d$, that is, the ratio $\beta \ll 1$, the critical frequency follows a quadratic relation with the \ac{MP} size \cite{Yellen2007}.

Equation \ref{eq:overdamped_oscillator} has a stable solution $\frac{d\phi}{d\tau}=0$ only when the driving frequency of the external magnetic field is less than $\omega_\mathrm{c}$ \cite{Yellen2007}, resulting in two distinct velocity regimes: 
\begin{equation}\label{eq:velocity_regimes}
    v = \begin{cases} 
\nu d,&  \nu \leq  \nu_{\mathrm{c}}(z)\\ 
(\nu - \sqrt{\nu^2 - \nu^2_\mathrm{{c}})}  d,&  \nu >  \nu_{\mathrm{c}}(z)
\end{cases},
\end{equation} 
which is depicted in Figure \ref{fig:two_velocity_regimes}.

\begin{figure}[bth]
    \centering

When $\nu \leq  \nu_{\mathrm{c}}(z)$, the \ac{MP} reaches a stable position in the potential energy minimum well and moves with the speed of the translation velocity of the potential energy landscape \cite{Yellen2007}: 
\begin{equation}
    \nu d.
\end{equation}
This regime is referred to as the phase-locked regime, in which the \ac{MP} lags behind the transformation of the landscape by a distance equal to\cite{Yellen2007}
\begin{equation}    
\Delta r = \left(\frac{d}{2\pi}\right)\sin^{-1} \frac{\nu}{\nu_\mathrm{c}}.
\end{equation}
Near $\nu_{\mathrm{c}}(z)$, the \ac{MP} will lag \SI{90}{\degree} out of phase with respect to the energy minimum, therefore the lag becomes the distance\cite{Yellen2007}
\begin{equation}
    \Delta r = \frac{d}{4}.
\end{equation}
It is only for $\nu > \nu_{\mathrm{c}}(z)$ that the \ac{MP} begins to slip with respect to the translating potential energy landscape \cite{Yellen2007}.
This is observable as an oscillatory rocking motion above a \ac{hh} or \ac{tt} \ac{DW}, and the time-averaged velocity of a \ac{MP} approaches 0 in accordance to Equation \ref{eq:velocity_regimes} for $\nu >  \nu_{\mathrm{c}}(z)$.

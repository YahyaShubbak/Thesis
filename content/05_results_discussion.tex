\subsection{Magnetophoretic velocity}
To ensure the \acp{MP} of different chemical functionalization have the same magnetic content and differences in the mean velocity are solely based on the electrostatic interactions, the magnetophoretic velocity far away from any wall has been measured.
This ensures that no surface-surface interaction occurs, and the velocity measured is based on the magnetic content.
To do so, a large fluid chamber was 3D printed where the \ac{MP} solution was placed of $\qtyproduct{11 x 11 x 2}{\milli\meter}$ with a bar magnet attached to one wall, which can be seen in \textbf{Figure \ref{fig:mag_phor_velocity_with_mag_field}} (b).

\begin{figure}[tbh]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\linewidth]{Abbildungen/Experimental/vMagPho.pdf}
    \caption{
            (a) Magnetic flux density in the investigated magnet orientation for different distances from the neodymium magnet attached to the wall at $x=0$.
            (b) Top view of the experimental setup using a neodymium magnet with a 3D-printed fluid chamber sealed by a coverslip (not shown).
            Taken from \cite{Shubbak2026}  in the Supplemental Material.
            }
    \label{fig:mag_phor_velocity_with_mag_field}
\end{figure}

To further ensure no wall interaction, the focal plane of the microscope was set to be in the center of the container. 
The magnetic gradient field attracts the \acp{MP} towards $x=0$, and this movement was captured with the camera. 
$B_\mathrm{x}(x)$ in dependence of the distance to the magnet can be seen in Figure \ref{fig:mag_phor_velocity_with_mag_field} (a).
The resulting mean magnetophoretic velocity $\left< \left< u \right> \right>$ was used to calculate the magnetic susceptibility $\chi_\mathrm{MP}$ using\cite{Wise2015}
\begin{equation}
    \left< \left< u \right> \right> = \frac{V(\chi_\mathrm{MP}-\chi_\mathrm{fl})}{6\pi r \eta}\frac{\left| \nabla B^2\right|}{2\mu_0},
\end{equation}
with the magnetic flux density $B_\mathrm{x}(x)$ of the bar magnet.
Videos with durations between \SI{10}{\s} and \SI{50}{\s} were recorded through the dark-field microscope with a 20$\,\times\,$ magnification objective. 
A total of 442 trajectories for \ch{COOH} and 155 trajectories for \ch{NH2} functionalized \acp{MP} of the micromod micromer®-M were evaluated as the \acp{MP} used in the mean velocity study of different surface functionalization.
At a distance of \SI{2}{mm} away from the magnet surface, i.e., at \SI{88.0}{\mT}, $\left< \left< u_\mathrm{COOH} \right> \right> = \SI{149\pm16}{\micro\m\per\s}$ and $\left< \left< u_\mathrm{NH2} \right> \right> = \SI{151\pm14}{\micro\m\per\s}$ were found. 
The resulting $\chi_\mathrm{MP}$ are $\chi_\mathrm{\ch{COOH}} = 0.26\pm0.01$ and $\chi_\mathrm{\ch{NH2}} = 0.26\pm0.01$, which is comparable to literature\cite{Grob2018}.

\section{Transport dynamics close to the surface}
\subsection{Mean ensemble velocity}\label{subsection:mean_velocity}
In this Section, the mean velocity $\left< v \right>$ of the particle ensemble transported close to the substrate's surface will be the main focus. 
It will be presented how the mean velocity is calculated considering a constant video duration, but with a varying number of steps each particle can take, or with a constant number of steps but, therefore, a varying video duration.
It will be shown that keeping the number of steps constant has benefits while granting a reasonably high statistic.
The used \acp{MP} are the micromer®-M particles from micromod with $r=\SI{1}{\micro\m}$ and either a carboxygroup or a mixture of a carboxygroup and an aminegroup at their respective surfaces.   

\subsubsection{Ratchet-like transport}
Using pulsed field traveling wave magnetophoresis (see Paragraph \ref{para:PFTWM}) with a distinct trapizoidal field shape, the magnetic potential energy landscape transformation creates a transport that is observable as a ratchet jumping motion.
In the following, different behaviours of the particles in the linear and beyond the linear regime will be discussed.
The graphs will present a single \ch{COOH}-micromer \ac{MP} that was transported and tracked the entire duration of the video, that is, $t=\SI{5}{s}$, or close to that.


Due to the transport mechanism, four distinct parts are present:
First, a positive field in $x$ gives an initial tilt above a \ac{DW} along the transport direction.
This initial transformation of the energy landscape pushes the \ac{MP} for a short distance, usually less than  \SI{2}{\micro\m}, appearing as a small step.
Then, the applied $z$ field changes polarity, and the superposition of the magnetic fields creates new minima at the adjacent \ac{DW}.
Accordingly, an energy maximum is now present at the \ac{DW} where the \ac{MP} was residing, which pushes the particle physically up.
Since the landscape is slightly skewed in the direction of transport by the applied $x$ field, the \ac{MP} does not randomly fall onto one or the other adjacent potential minima, but it has a preferred direction along $x$.
This is now observable as a large step which, in sum with the small step, will cover $\frac{1}{2}d$, that is, the distance from one \ac{DW} to the next.

\paragraph{Linear transport regime}
When driving frequencies below a certain value are applied, and the \acp{MP} are able to closely follow the dynamically changing potential energy landscape, i.e., the \acp{MP} are in the phase locked regime, the mean velocity of the \acp{MP} increase lineraly accoding to Equation \ref{eq:velocity_regimes}.  
In an $x$ over $t$ graph, the two steps are separated by a stationary phase. 
Such a graph is presented in Figure \ref{fig:x-t-graph_100ms}.
The red rectangle is shown as a zoom in in Figure \ref{fig:x-t-graph_100ms_zoom_velocity} (b) to more clearly present the individual steps and how they are correlated with the alternating pulsed field sequence, depicted on top in (a).

$\mathrm{T}$ is set to start arbitrarily at $t=\SI{2200}{ms}$ to include a full period in the scheme; however, the field sequence started before that. 
Note that the ramp time transitioning between $\pm H_\mathrm{max}$ shown is exaggerated for visual purposes.
A full period $\mathrm{T}$ containing the aforementioned small step starting at $t=\SI{2200}{ms}$, and the large step starting at $t=\SI{2300}{ms}$, is shown.
$\mathrm{T} = \SI{400}{ms}$ can be nicely observed, since $\nicefrac{T}{4}=\SI{100}{ms}$. 


The force equilibrium responsible for the directed transport is reached within nanoseconds (refer to theory or cite Holzinger?) which is not resolvable at the \si{ms} time scale the camera can provide.
Therefore, the acceleration phase is assumed to be instantaneous and the force equilibrium between $F_\mathrm{m,x}$ and $\mathbf{F}_\mathrm{D}(z,t)$ is reached immediately.
The change from $+H_\mathrm{z, max}$ to $-H_\mathrm{z, max}$ pushes the \ac{MP} physically up the newly created potential energy maxima. 
After the instantaneous acceleration, the particle moves linearly by falling down the potential well, and is then decelerated before it comes to a stop at the adjacent \ac{DW}.
One explanation for the deceleration would be the change in height during a step as it falls:
while falling, $f_\mathrm{D}$ increases as a funciton if $z$ accodring to Equation \ref{eq:f_D}, increasing $\mathbf{F}_\mathrm{D}(z,t)$ (Equation \ref{eq:drag_force}).
$\Delta z$ is several \si{\micro\m}\cite{Huhnstock2022}, therefore significant enough for a change to be visible.
Far away, that is, several $\si{\micro \m}$ above the surface, $f_\mathrm{D}=1$, and will not influence the drag, thus a perceived linear velocity is observed. 
Approaching the surface will increase $f_\mathrm{D}>1$, increasing Stokes' drag and deceleration is observed.
Qualitatively, this is closer to a linear rather than an exponential decay in velocity. 
This is especially visible in Figure \ref{fig:x-t-graph_100ms_zoom_velocity} (c) in the derivative of the smoothed data at both large steps, that is, from \SI{2290}{ms} to \SI{2315}{ms} and \SI{2490}{ms} to \SI{2515}{ms}.  
This further corroborates the deceleration due to drag increase rather than a decrease in magnetic field strength as the \ac{MP} moves sideways away from the greatest field gradient. 

Further, an asymmetric stripe width is present.
This is explained by the difference in \ch{He+}-ion bombarded stripes and those kept pristine. 
After bombardment, this and that happen, causing a broadening of one domain over the other.
While $d$ is still $\SI{10}{\micro\m}$ within the error margin, individual stripes have an asymmetry.
In the depicted case of Figure \ref{fig:x-t-graph_100ms_zoom_velocity}, the asyymetrie is 
\begin{equation}
    \text{Asymmetrie} = \frac{\text{Stripe}_1 - \text{Stripe}_2}{\text{Stripe}_1 + \text{Stripe}_2} = \frac{\SI{5.36}{\micro\m} - \SI{4.70}{\micro\m}}{\SI{5.36}{\micro\m} + \SI{4.70}{\micro\m}} = \SI{6.56}{\percent},
\end{equation}
and the bombarded stripe is wider than its unbombarded counterpart (see section He Bombardement).

\paragraph{Non-linear regime}
After the critical frequency $\nu_\mathrm{c}$, the mean ensemble velocity follows a decay according to Equation \ref{eq:velocity_regimes}, which can be seen in Figure \ref{fig:two_velocity_regimes}. 
In this regime, \acp{MP} can have different behaviours, since in real-world applications \acp{MP} are not perfectly uniform.
This means that larger \acp{MP} with more magnetic content might still be in the phase-locked regime and move parallel to the co-moving potential energy landscape, while slightly smaller ones experience slips back, and the smallest particles only oscillate above a given \ac{DW}.


Slips back of one \ac{MP} can be seen in Figure \ref{fig:x-t-graph_20ms}. 
If in the linear regime, the \ac{MP} could have traveld \SI{625}{\micro\m} in \SI{5}{s} at \SI{12.5}{Hz} according to  
\begin{equation}\label{eq:traj_length}
    x = \frac{t}{\mathrm{T/2}}\frac{d}{2} = t\cdot 2\nu \cdot  \frac{d}{2} = \SI{5000}{ms} \cdot  2 \cdot \SI{12.5}{mHz} \cdot  \frac{\SI{10}{\micro\m}}{2} = \SI{625}{\micro \m}. 
\end{equation}
Due to the afformentoined slips, the \ac{MP} only managed to cover $\approx\SI{175}{\micro\m}$.


A feature of the driving software becomes visible.
The phase relation between $H_\mathrm{x, max}$ and $H_\mathrm{z, max}$ is inverted after $\mathrm{T} =20$. 
As a consequence, transport in the $+x$ direction is followed by transport in the $-x$ direction and so on.
Since the video duration in this experiment series was fixed, this phase relation change is only visible after FIND OUT CALCULATION Hz.
This is especially well visible when the observed \ac{MP} only oscillates above a \ac{DW}, as is the case in Figure \ref{fig:x-t-graph_20ms}.
A \ac{MP} transported in $+x$ is concidered (Figure \ref{fig:x-t-graph_20ms} at $t=\SI{0}{ms}$) with the coordinate system placed that the center of it has its 0 point in the center of the \ac{DW}.
The \ac{MP} resides above that \ac{DW}, with the effective field pushing it toward the $+x$-edge.
It has too much inertia to follow the moving potential energy landscape and is only able to follow the change in $\pm H_\mathrm{x, max}$, leading to a back and forth movement above the point where it is pinned, of about \SI{1}{\micro\m}, visible as sharp peaks in the $x-t$-graph.
This follows that with each $T/2$, that is, when $H_\mathrm{x, max}$ changes sign, the \ac{MP} is pushed from left to right. 
Since this happens \SI{20}{T} times, a total of 20 peaks towards the $+x$ and 20 peaks towards $-x$ are visible.
After that, the phze relation changes.
Now the \ac{MP} is pushed to the $-x$ edge of the \ac{DW}.
This is visible at $t\approx \SI{1100}{ms}$ as the \ac{MP} is covering a larger distance of about \SI{1.5}{\micro\m}.
Then, the same scheme of \SI{1}{\micro\m} peaks happens for a total of 40 times before the phase relation changes once more. 

Figure \ref{fig:x-t-graph_20ms_zoom} has $\mathrm{T}$ depicted.
It demonstrates unpredictable behaviour as the number of slips back can not be predicted when the properties of the \acp{MP} vary.

\paragraph{Velocity calcullation}
To calculate the velocity, filtering steps are performed as described in Paragraph Filtering steps of Chapter \ref{paragraph:trackpy_filtering}.
Then, $\frac{dx}{dt}$ is derived for a given trajectory of an \ac{MP} - this is the mean velocity $\left< v \right>$ of one \ac{MP} across the duration $t$.
The mean of $\left< v \right>$ of all \acp{MP} gives then the mean of the mean $\left< \left< v \right> \right>$ from which also the number of velocity measurements is derived.
Done for all given driving frequencies $\nu$ yields the $\left< \left< v \right> \right>$ over $\nu$ graph that can be seen in Figure \ref{fig:MeanVelocityCOOHNH2}.
The number of velocity measurements per frequency is given as a color map represented in each data point.
Finally, to infer $\nu_\mathrm{c}$ from the graph, Equation \ref{eq:velocity_regimes} is fitted by separating the data points into the linear fit and the decaying regime and approaching with a least square fit the best parameters.
This yields $\nu_\mathrm{c}$ per graph, which is also given in the figure.
Error bars in Figure \ref{fig:MeanVelocityCOOHNH2} represent a confidence level of \SI{95}{\percent} calculated 
using the standard error of the mean ($SEM$) multiplied by the critical value $Z=1.96$ for large $n$, where $\mathrm{SEM} =\nicefrac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}$, whith $\sigma$ being the sample standard deviation and $n$ being the sample size.

\section{Transport of magnetic particles bound to proteins}
A major step towards a useful \ac{LOC} system is to prove that \acp{MP} can, indeed, be distinguished in their transport properties once bound to molecules. 
The following chapter will answer the question of how to efficiently transport proteins bound to particles and, consequently, how to detect that binding.
A mean velocity analysis similar to the one shown in Section \ref{subsection:mean_velocity} will be presented further, cementing the feasibility of the transport and detection concept.
The influence of different protein concentrations will be discussed, comparing Zeta potentials and the mean ensemble transport velocities.

\chapter{Introduction}
In December 2019, the world faced a pandemic outbreak of proportions difficult to imagine \cite{Ciotti2020,Vandenberg2020}.
The COVID-19 outbreak caused a global shutdown, with a high toll in terms of human lives lost, economic repercussions, and increased poverty \cite{Ciotti2020}. 
Four months after the outbreak, the first spike of 50 thousand deaths in a single week was achieved, overshadowed by a later peak of 104 thousand deaths in a single week in January 2021, accumulating to over seven million worldwide as of March 2026, exactly six years later  https://data.who.int/dashboards/covid19/deaths.
Development of tests for the SARS-CoV-2 virus to prevent further spread was of stark interest \cite{Vandenberg2020}.  
While elaborate testing schemes such as the use of \ac{PCR}, CRISPR-Cas12-based lateral flow assay, or serology were available early on \cite{Ciotti2020,Vandenberg2020}, these tests could not be scaled to cover a wide number of the population at once to effectively contain the spread, as these specialized techniques both take time and rely on modern infrastructure, expensive medical equipment, and trained laboratory staff \cite{Vandenberg2020}, which are often limited to more developed nations with a working medical system. 
One breakthrough was the rapid testing developed in fall 2020, which, for the first time, allowed for individual widespread testing \cite{Vandenberg2020}.

The pandemic aside, detection of a manifold of analytes ranging from viruses \cite{SanchezGalan2021,Verheyen2015,Nique2021,Vandenberg2020} to proteins \cite{Szabo2015,Gajdosova2021}, or biomarkers in cancer cells \cite{Topkaya2016,Li2012,Wu2015}, is often a costly and time-consuming procedure.
The search for new diagnostics is consequently highly desired \cite{Haleem2021,Oyewole2021,Vandenberg2020} and has sparked a great branch of research in the last decades \cite{Reginka2021,Knight2002,Kricka2001,Holland2005, Ehresmann2015,Wang2014}.
Figure \ref{fig:number_publications_POC} illustrates just that: in the last 35 years, the number of publications concerning \ac{POCT} has significantly increased, almost 18-fold, from a combined 240 publications in the field in 1990 to over 4230 publications in 2021.

\ac{LOC} devices are designed with the overall objective of integrating one or more laboratory functions into a chip having merely a size of a few millimeters to centimeters \cite{Schoenberger2016}. 
From the perspective of a medical device, \acp{LOC} may be seen as a scaled-down version of in-vitro diagnostics \cite{Schoenberger2016}. 
They can be mass-produced cheaply and thus allow, for example, the detection of diseases in third-world countries \cite{Schoenberger2016}.
These regions frequently face a rudimentary health-care system while being simultaneously impacted by plagues such as malaria, hepatitis, AIDS, or Ebola, which could be made detectable using \ac{LOC} \cite{Schoenberger2016}.  
In affluent societies, accessing \ac{LOC} technologies makes it possible to monitor socially stigmatized illnesses, which may lead to more effective and quicker disease treatment \cite{Schoenberger2016}. 

While no doubt a vital part in disease control, \ac{POCT} diagnostic tools such as SARS-CoV-2 rapid self-tests are often single-use or at least contain a single-use component, and are designed for a specific disease.
Such tests that have been used for decades now include pregnancy tests, tests for sugar levels in diabetic monitoring, and blood tests for rheumatism.

\ac{POCT} using magnetic particles is one emerging field. 
They are employed in so-called \ac{LOC} devices.
There, they promise various advantages over their paper-chromatography-based counterparts in standard \ac{POCT} devices. 
For once, the substrate, consisting of a magnetic stray field creating layer, is reusable and acts as the base of the device.
The \acp{MP}, which take up the role as the probes, are functionalized chemically on their polymer-coated surface that enables them to interact with different chemical groups. 
This means that after a given test is performed on one given device and the substrate is rinsed with water, a second test can be performed right away, allowing for many types of \acp{MP} to perform very different tests. 
As the quantity of \acp{MP} used is minute, this keeps the cost of each test low.
Different approaches using these magnetic \ac{LOC} systems are being researched. 
For the magnetic stray field creation, micromagnets are used, electrical wires for electromagnetic creation, and even...
These, however, are topographically elevated elements, limiting these devices to \ac{MP} transport far away from the substrate's surface. 
The emerging magnetic stray field gradient needed to exert a magnetic force on the particles is washed out and not well defined, limiting the transport to... forces such as DLVO   
Topographically flat substrates, however, allow the \acp{MP} to be close to the surface, where they see a much more defined and stronger magnetic field gradient, additionally opening up the surface sensitive \ac{DLVO} forces such as electrostatic (repulsion) and van der Waals (attraction).

The work presented in this thesis focuses on the physical and chemical interactions only possible by the surface close transport of the \acp{MP} on topographically flat magnetic substrates, where the key difference in interaction is the electrostatic repulsion that, at the distances of xxx micrometer, greatly influences the drag felt by the \acp{MP} which, in turn, greatly change the transport dynamics of two \ac{MP} species.
